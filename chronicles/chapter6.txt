Chapter VI — The Page Beneath the World
Mistbarrow does not easily release those who begin to understand it. Even after leaving its halls, its questions linger like ink that refuses to dry, pressing gently but persistently against the mind. It was perhaps inevitable, then, that the companions would return — not as students, nor as guests, but as seekers drawn back by something unfinished beneath the academy’s foundations.
The familiar warmth of Mistbarrow greeted them once more, though it felt thinner now, like a surface stretched over something deeper. Life continued within its walls as though nothing had changed. Lex slipped easily back into the rhythm of the place, laughter and charm following him from table to table as he entertained himself among students and scholars alike. Luna, meanwhile, was drawn almost immediately toward the promise of the library, her thoughts consumed by preservation, by the fragile defiance of ink against time. Pip alone seemed out of place in the quiet comfort of the academy. The weight of stone, voices, and questions pressed heavily upon him, and more than anything, he longed for rest.
Yet it was Pip who remembered what the others had overlooked.
The kitchen. The scent of flour. The quiet, forgotten door.
It stood where he had seen it before — plain, crude, almost deliberately unremarkable. But beyond it lay a staircase, descending further than any part of the academy should allow. The air cooled with each step, growing heavier, as though the deeper they went, the more the world above struggled to follow.
Soon, the stone itself began to change.
The walls softened, not by age, but by design. Layers of parchment had been pressed and glued together until the cavern seemed lined with words rather than rock. Scraps of lecture notes were pinned into place, corrected in red ink by careful, relentless hands. At the center of the chamber stood a small wooden table with two cups resting upon it, untouched, as if a conversation had once taken place here and never reached its conclusion. The air felt thick, not with dust, but with the lingering impression of thought, as though words had been flattened and preserved within the walls themselves.
Pip did not hesitate. With a sudden, decisive motion, he drove his foot into the crude wooden door set into the alcove. The wood splintered easily, collapsing inward as though it had long since lost the will to resist.
Beyond it, the deeper truth revealed itself.
Shelves curved along the natural stone, carved directly into the cavern walls. Yet what they held was not knowledge preserved, but knowledge dismantled. Scrolls had been taken apart, separated into fragments and sorted into bundles labeled Thesis, Context, and Counterpoint. The last of these stood nearly empty. Meaning itself had been reduced, broken down into pieces, its opposition stripped away until only a single narrative remained.
Floating candles drifted lazily through the air, their wax rising instead of falling, as if even the laws of the world had begun to loosen in this place. Among the fragments, patterns emerged — repeated references to water, to depth, to northern lakes. One name appeared again and again, quietly insistent: Lake Virienne.
They pressed onward, unwilling to linger where thought itself had been unmade.
The next chamber opened into a broader cavern, its low ceiling darkened by soot and time. Wooden crates and bundled scrolls were stacked in careful rows along the walls, some bound in black ribbon, others in red. The air carried the scent of damp parchment and drying ink, and the faint crackle of that ink was the only sound that broke the stillness. Everything here felt deliberate, organized, yet entirely devoid of reverence — as though knowledge had been catalogued only to be controlled.
In the chamber beyond, that control took shape more violently.
A long stone table stretched through the center, stained black and red from years of scraping, cutting, and rewriting. Torn strips of parchment lay scattered across its surface like discarded skin. On the far wall, a mural spread across the rock, depicting a city caught between destruction and erasure — half consumed by fire, half dissolving into water, all of it violently crossed out in red ink. Along its edge, a single note had been written with quiet certainty: Fire misleads. Depth conceals.
They studied the mural for a long time, searching for meaning where none willingly revealed itself. It was Luna who reached beyond sight, threading memory through sound, allowing something half-forgotten to surface. Through her, Pip felt recognition stir — a distant place, somewhere north of his forest, known not through travel, but through the quiet language of instinct. Still, the truth remained hidden. Until fire touched the wall.
The painted image burned away under the flame, revealing not destruction, but concealment. Beneath the mural lay a sealed door, marked by three mechanisms. Together, without hesitation, they pressed them at once. The stone shifted, and the path opened.
Cold air rushed out to meet them.
The chamber beyond was raw, untouched, its ceiling low enough to force them to bow as they entered. Five stones jutted from the far wall, each carved with a symbol: flame, wave, leaf, eye, spiral. Above them, etched deep into the rock, a question waited: What survived the fire?
The cold here was unnatural, biting through thought as much as flesh. It was not a place that invited reflection. It demanded it. And so, instead of answering, they fled forward, choosing motion over stillness.
The next cavern widened abruptly, its silence broken only by the faint tremble of cords stretched between stone pillars. From these cords hung fragments of bone and tiny skulls, each no larger than a fist, interwoven with scraps of parchment that fluttered like suspended thoughts. The air carried a faint scent of dust and old marrow.
As they stepped forward, the cords trembled.
From behind the rocks, the creatures emerged — small, skeletal forms marked with inked runes, their movements sharp and purposeful. When they spoke, it was in perfect unison, their voices thin and hollow.
“Reduce.”
The battle that followed was chaotic, but not desperate. Strength alone proved insufficient against such strange adversaries, yet where force faltered, magic prevailed. Flame, moss, water, and motion intertwined in unpredictable harmony, transforming the cavern into something almost absurd in its energy. Spells flared, laughter broke through tension, and one by one, the creatures fell apart into silence.
They did not remain to reflect on it. Something deeper still awaited.
The final chamber opened into vastness. Twelve stone pillars rose in a perfect circle, forming a space that felt less carved than declared. At its center hovered a sphere of impossible light, suspended above a spiral etched into the ground. It did not burn, nor did it shine. It folded inward, compressing itself again and again, as though containing something far too large to exist.
Lex did not wait. Curiosity carried him forward, quick and fearless, and before the others could speak, his foot crossed the spiral etched into the stone. The reaction was immediate. The light convulsed violently, folding inward upon itself. The pillars trembled. Every fragment of parchment tore free at once, whipping through the air in a storm of unraveling meaning. The space itself seemed to collapse, drawn toward a single point.
Lex turned once, his expression no longer playful, but clear with sudden understanding. “It isn’t destruction,” he said, the realization striking too late. “It’s compression.”
The blinding light imploded and silence followed.
For a long moment, neither Pip nor Luna moved, stunned and confused. Then, from the steady glow of the lantern, a whisper emerged — soft, distant, but unmistakable. “North.”
And when they opened them again, the world had changed.
The cavern, the pillars, the buried chambers of Mistbarrow — all had vanished. In their place stretched an endless field of green, grass rolling gently beneath a silent sky. No trees marked the horizon. No ruins broke the distance. Nothing moved but the wind.
