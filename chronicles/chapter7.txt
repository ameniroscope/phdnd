Chapter VII — Thellerkin Discovery
Pip and Luna opened their eyes beneath a sky that had no memory of stone. One moment they had stood in the buried shrine beneath Mistbarrow, where Lex had vanished into impossible light; the next, they found themselves in a field where no road led and no horizon offered comfort. Grass stretched in every direction, impossibly green, swaying in a breeze that carried no scent of earth, flower, beast, or rain. No insects hummed. No birds called. The world felt paused, as though it had waited untouched for their arrival.
Above them, the sky shone a flawless blue, too clear and bright to feel real. There was no trace of the cavern, no spiral carved into stone, no fractured portal, no mark to explain how they had come there. It was as if the moment before had been wiped clean from the world. Only one thing broke the horizon: a small hut standing far away against the endless green. It did not seem closer no matter how long they looked, yet it remained there, fixed and solitary, the only promise in a world stripped bare.
Then the lantern stirred.
Its light flickered weakly, trembling as though the flame inside were struggling to remember itself. From within came a familiar voice, thin and uncertain.
“...still here. Mostly. I think.”
For a moment, Pip and Luna could only stare. So much had happened so quickly — the shrine, the light, the field, the impossible silence — that grief itself had barely found time to arrive. But now it did. Lex was gone. Truly gone. Not merely lost around a corner or carried off by mischief, not hiding with a clever smile and some stolen trinket in hand. His voice lingered only as something fragile within the lantern, stretched thin between presence and absence.
They held close to one another, shaken and bewildered, while the hut waited in the distance.
With no other path before them, they began to walk. At first, the field seemed gentle beneath their feet. The grass bent softly around their steps, and the air moved with calm indifference. Yet the longer they walked, the stranger the land became. The green deepened, the blades thickened, and the ground beneath them grew dense and heavy, as though the world itself were becoming more alive the closer they came to the hut.
Then Pip sank.
What had seemed firm a moment before softened beneath him like wet soil. His foot vanished, then his leg, and soon the earth pulled at him with slow, deliberate hunger. He clawed at grass, roots, anything that might hold, earth gathering beneath his nails and fur as Luna reached for him with alarm. For a frightening moment, the field felt less like land than a sleeping creature turning in its dreams. But Pip struggled, and Luna answered with magic and desperate hands, until at last he was pulled free, shaking and covered in soil.
When he stood again, something within him had changed. Perhaps the field had touched him. Perhaps his own bond with root and spore had deepened from fear into understanding. Whatever the cause, Pip could now feel the life around him differently — its richness, its emptiness, its wrongness. The land was no longer only something beneath his feet. It was a voice, faint but present, waiting to be asked the right question.
They continued more carefully after that, though caution did not save them from the field’s next warning. Vines snapped upward without warning, coiling around their ankles with controlled strength. The more they resisted, the tighter the vines held, not breaking bone, not drawing blood, but making clear that they could. Yet Pip and Luna did not answer the field with violence. They spoke instead. They explained that they were lost, that they sought understanding, that they had come from a place of danger and meant no harm to whatever guarded the hut. For a long breath, nothing changed. Then the vines loosened.
One by one, they slipped back beneath the grass, leaving Pip and Luna standing at last before the hut.
It was small, built of aged wood that had been carefully maintained rather than restored. Inside, it held only a single room, modest in size but filled with quiet intention. No dust rested on the beams. No rot touched the walls. Light entered through a narrow window, soft and pale, revealing shelves lined with vials, herbs, roots, bark, parchment, and stoppered bottles whose liquids had settled into stillness long ago. Nothing appeared abandoned. Everything had been placed with care, as though the hut were not empty, but waiting. On one parchment they found a riddle beside six colored potions. Its meaning remained stubbornly hidden, and after some debate they chose not to tempt every mystery in the room. 
Another riddle waited near the table beneath the window, where old ink stains and circular marks showed that objects had often rested there.
I hold mountains, rivers, and roads,
yet none of them move.
I show distant places,
though I never leave the room.
The answer came easily: a map.
When the word was spoken aloud, something clicked within the hut. A hidden compartment opened, revealing a map tucked safely away, preserved as if by purpose rather than chance. It was old, but not useless. Another fragment. Another direction. Another piece of the world as it had once been.
Then came movement.
A child darted from the back of the hut and fled before they could speak. Pip and Luna gave chase, but they had scarcely reached the trees before a great squirrel burst upon them, fierce and furious in defense of the boy. They tried kindness first — words, food, patience — but fear had made the creature wild. At last Pip released his strange spores, not to harm but to soothe, and the squirrel’s fury melted into strange delight. In that moment, the boy saw what mattered most: they had not killed his guardian.
So he took their hands and he led them through the forest, along paths that did not seem to exist until he stepped upon them, through curtains of leaf and root and shadow, until the trees opened not into a village, but into a city grown from the living wood.
Thellerkin.
It rose in layers among enormous trunks, a forest city of bridges, huts, platforms, and warm golden light. Homes were carved not from dead timber but into living trees, shaped gently into windows, doors, and chambers. Wooden walkways stretched from trunk to trunk, some wide enough for carts, others narrow and swaying. Rope lifts carried baskets and supplies upward through the canopy. Everywhere, small figures moved with purpose, their village calm yet alive, busy yet strangely peaceful. Nothing here had been built against the forest. Everything belonged to it.
Their arrival drew attention quickly, and soon they were brought before the clan leader — a grand, ridiculous, magnificently self-important figure who seemed delighted by the opportunity to become important in front of strangers. Pip and Luna explained as best they could: the cult beneath Mistbarrow, the search for Archivon, the portal, the field, the hut, the map. The leader listened with theatrical gravity, clearly pleased that destiny had chosen to arrive at his door.
He swept them into the heart of Thellerkin with great ceremony, eager above all to show them what he considered the pride of his people: the library.
It was vast beyond expectation.
Shelves rose through the living trunks, winding upward in spirals of carved wood and woven platforms. Books, scrolls, tablets, bark pages, preserved leaves, and bound bundles filled the space with the scent of ink, sap, dust, and green life. This was no mere collection. It was a rescued memory, a forest-grown answer to destruction. Here, the Thellerkin had kept what others had lost.
Yet at first, answers remained slippery. The leader boasted more than he explained, and many of their questions vanished beneath his pride. Only when Pip and Luna brought out their maps did the tone change. An ancient cartographer was summoned, old and bent, his eyes brightening the moment he saw the parchment. He studied the two maps with reverence and astonishment, explaining that they came from different ages. Their differences were not mistakes, but evidence — proof that the land had shifted, that records had changed, that memory itself had layers.
In that library, surrounded by the rescued remnants of old knowledge, another truth came into focus. The Thellerkin believed that Archivon had burned. Not merely damaged, not hidden, not sleeping beneath water, but burned down with its great library. Long ago, the great-great-grandfather of the current chief had helped save what books could be saved from that ruin, carrying knowledge into the forest before ash claimed the rest. This, they said, was why Thellerkin possessed such a library. Not because they stole from Archivon, as the cult believed, but because they had saved what they could.
Still, uncertainty remained. If Archivon had burned, why did so many signs point toward water? If the city had been destroyed, why did maps, spirits, and lanterns continue to whisper of it as though it still endured?
While Luna continued to seek answers, Pip slipped quietly among the shelves, drawn by curiosity and perhaps by need. There, he found a book that caught his eye — and with great secrecy, placed it in his bag. Whether this was theft, rescue, or destiny wearing a smaller cloak, only time would tell.
Their path then led them to an old potion-maker, a woman delighted beyond measure to meet travelers willing to help with a mystery of her own. She had long struggled to understand a spell left behind by her sister, its meaning tangled in old notes and careful measurements. Pip and Luna lent their minds and hands to the work, helping her complete what had remained unfinished. In gratitude, she gave them two gifts: a truth potion, clear in purpose, and another elixir whose nature remained unknown.
Not all in Thellerkin welcomed them so warmly.
As they walked through the village, a young boy confronted them with anger sharp enough to wound. He accused them of belonging to the cult, of bringing danger, of being tied somehow to the disappearance of his brother months before. He wanted a fight. Perhaps he wanted someone to blame. Perhaps grief had left him no room for anything else.
Pip and Luna did not meet his anger with anger.
They spoke gently. They told him what they knew. They explained that the cult was their enemy too, and that they had come not to steal, but to understand. Slowly, reluctantly, the boy’s fury loosened. He did not forgive them, not truly. Such wounds do not close with one conversation. But he walked away without raising a hand.
By then, exhaustion had settled over them like dusk.
The field, the hut, the village, the library, the maps, the accusations, Lex’s fragile voice still flickering in the lantern — all of it pressed upon them. They had found the Thellerkin at last, and with them a new version of Archivon’s story: not a drowned city, but a burned one; not lost knowledge, but rescued memory.
Yet the contradictions only deepened.
And so, before chasing the next truth, Pip and Luna chose what all seekers eventually must and rested.
