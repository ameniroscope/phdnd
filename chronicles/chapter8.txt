Chapter VIII — The Lake That Remembers
Morning came softly to Thellerkin, filtering gold and green through the canopy, and for a while Pip and Luna simply lay still among the woven platforms, listening to the forest wake around them. Rope-lifts creaked. Somewhere below, a kettle sang. The village stirred the way a living thing stirs — unhurried, certain of its own rhythm.
It was the boy who found them first. He came without anger this time, his earlier fury folded away somewhere out of sight, replaced by the restless energy of a child who had decided, for now, that they were not enemies. He talked quickly, as though he had been saving up things to say and had only just been given permission to say them. There was a mosaic weaver, he told them, an old woman who worked tiles into pictures that seemed to shift when no one was looking directly at them. She spoke of a secret lake — not one of the four that shaped the land as travelers knew it, but something older, something that held every secret ever lost to water. He warned them, half-proud and half-uncertain, that she would not simply tell her stories; she would only share them with those who could prove they deserved to listen. There was also the witch, he said, lowering his voice as if the word itself required care. She brewed things that did not always behave the way brewing things ought to. And to the east, he added, almost as an afterthought, there were lakes — small ones, quiet ones. He did not know much about them, only that the older Thellerkin spoke of them the way one speaks of a room best left with the door closed.
Pip and Luna exchanged a glance. It was, by now, a familiar kind of morning.
They found the weaver exactly where the boy said they would, tucked into a hollow between two roots thick as doorways, surrounded by baskets of colored tile. She did not look up when they approached, only continued fitting small glazed pieces into a pattern that seemed, at first glance, to be nothing at all. "The lake remembers what people forget," she said, before either of them had spoken a word. "Everyone forgets. That is what makes it so full." She spoke of a secret lake, hidden apart from Lake Virienne and its silvered kin — a place, she claimed, where every secret that had ever slipped from memory eventually came to rest, sinking gently until the water held more truth than the world above it ever had. She would say no more, not yet. First, she said, they would have to prove their minds were worth trusting with such things, and she gestured to the tiles.
The game was simple in the telling and considerably less simple in the doing — a memory trial of pattern and recall, tiles flipped and studied and flipped again, colors and shapes that had to be held in mind long after they were turned face-down. Luna's discipline of cataloguing and memorization served her well; Pip's instincts, sharper than he gave them credit for, filled in what her mind lost track of. Between them, slowly, painstakingly, they matched what needed matching. The weaver studied the completed mosaic for a long moment when they finished, her expression unreadable. "Good," she said simply. And then, at last, she told them what she knew — fragments of the secret lake, its whereabouts spoken of only in riddles passed between the old and the trusted, its waters said to hold not just memory, but meaning itself, waiting for someone patient enough to look. It was not a map. But it was, unmistakably, a beginning.
From there they made their way to the witch's hut, which smelled of scorched herbs and something faintly metallic. She greeted them with the particular delight of someone who enjoyed visitors mostly for the excuse to test things on them. "I have something," she said, holding up a small vial with theatrical care. "It will either loosen the tongue entirely — every truth, spilling out like water from a cracked jug — or —" she paused, clearly relishing this part, "— it will grow you a rather impressive extra arm." Neither Pip nor Luna found this especially reassuring. "I haven't tested it," the witch admitted cheerfully, as if this were a minor detail rather than the entire point. "But I'm sure it's one or the other. Possibly both, if you're unlucky. Or lucky. Depends on the arm." She pressed the vial into Luna's hands regardless, along with a knowing look that suggested she considered the matter settled, and sent them on their way with the vague good humor of someone who fully expected to hear how the story ended eventually.
They left Thellerkin's canopy behind and walked until the trees thinned into open ground, where a handful of small lakes lay scattered like dropped coins, still and dark beneath the morning light. There was nothing dramatic about them — no mist, no waiting spirits, no obvious warning. Only water, quiet and untroubled. Which was, in its own way, exactly what made touching one so unsettling. The moment skin met surface, the world folded away. What followed was not quite sleep and not quite waking — a drifting, dreamlike stretch of mist and shifting ground, a maze that seemed built from memory rather than stone, its walls forming and dissolving as though the lake itself were still deciding what shape the truth ought to take. Pip and Luna wandered it for what felt like a very long time, the details of that place folding strangely, half-remembered even as they were lived.
At the heart of it, after all that wandering, mist, and quiet dread, there was a map. Only a map. Luna did not bother hiding his frustration. She had half expected revelation, or danger, or some fragment of Archivon's endless riddle. Instead, the lake had handed them cartography, folded neatly, as though the secret it guarded was simply this: that even secrets sometimes only point toward more walking. Pip, more measured, tucked it away thinking that maybe this map could bring them to archivon. 
The walk back to Thellerkin should have been quiet. It was not. The great squirrel found them again on the forest path — the same fierce guardian they had once soothed with Pip's spores, now clearly less inclined to forgive and forget. It came at them with the particular fury of a creature that remembered exactly who had confused it the first time and had spent the intervening days deciding how it felt about that. The fight was short and chaotic, claws and fur and Pip ducking behind roots he was fairly sure hadn't been growing in his way a moment ago. In the scramble, someone — no one would later agree on whose idea it truly was — decided the witch's untested vial deserved its first trial by fire.
The squirrel drank it.
For one very long second, nothing happened. Then an arm — enormous, sudden, and entirely unasked-for — burst from the creature's side, flailing with the confused violence of a limb that had existed for less than three seconds and already deeply resented the world it had been born into. Whatever the vial was, it was decisively not veritaserum. Pip, seizing the only sensible option available to him, unleashed his spores once more. The squirrel, already reeling from its unexpected new limb, staggered under the added weight of drifting hallucination and fled crashing into the underbrush, arm and all, leaving the pair alone on the path with nothing but adrenaline and the faint, ridiculous echo of what they had just witnessed.
Neither of them said much on the rest of the walk back. By the time Thellerkin's lights came into view through the trees, exhaustion had caught up with them fully — the lake, the maze, the map, the arm, all of it pressing down at once. They found quiet corners among the platforms and let sleep take them before deciding, come morning, which road deserved to be walked next.
