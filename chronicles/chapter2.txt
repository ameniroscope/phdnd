Chapter II – From Logaros to Fenkrey
The road wound gently northward, sunlight spilling like honey between the trees. Master Aldorren, ever the performer, filled the quiet with his booming voice as he spun tales of the lost city.
“Archivon,” he declared, “the city of cities, the vault of every truth worth knowing! Some say it burned, others insist it drowned, still others whisper it was stolen by the fae. But the truth, my companions, is this: Archivon was not destroyed, not entirely. Its wisdom trickled out like seeds in the wind. Gather enough of those seeds… and the tree of Archivon might sprout again.”
Luna and Pip shared a glance — already wary of Aldorren’s love for his own voice. Still, the myth intrigued them.
During a well deserved break, Luna, sitting under the trees to read her spellbook, was approached by a hunched alchemist who noticed her notes. His voice shook as he whispered: “Archivon never burned — it drowned. Water poured into its halls like serpents, yet the paper remembered.” Pressing a vial of still water into her hands, he warned: “It glows when truth is near. Be ready to pay the price.”  Luna clutched it tightly, thrilled by the gift.
Not long after, a sudden gust scattered Luna’s messy notes across a stone bridge. She chased after them desperately, but later realized one page was still missing. She sets out in the deep of the forest hoping to find the missing page. While alone on her quest, Luna was ambushed by a cloaked wizard who bent time itself — clocks spinning, stones shattering. After a desperate struggle, she triumphed, claiming his strange binding tome, a notebook where any page would fuse itself forever. Luna, ecstatic, began to imagine the treasures she might store there.
Meanwhile, Pip had wandered into the forest and faced down a hoard of goblins. Triumphant, he returned bearing the head of their leader. Yet his victory soured quickly when he discovered his beloved mushroom snacks stolen by a sly trickster. The thief demanded a trade: the goblin head for the snacks. With a heavy heart, Pip chose the snacks — and promptly devoured them in celebration.
On her way to camp, Luna finally catches a glimpse of the page she was looking for under a stone. What she recovered, however, was something stranger: a page written in an unknown hand, describing a spell to preserve plants within glass, the Vivarium Spell.
That night, the company rested in a cozy roadside inn. Pip sang with his enchanted hat, Aldorren wove tales for delighted patrons, and Luna won a clever game of cards. Wanting to remember the moment, Luna kept a small trinket — a broken clock — a perfect keepsake for her growing collection of memory-objects.
The next day, they finally arrive at Fenkrey, the next stop on Aldorren’s journey of knowledge diffusion. Drawn to Luna’s trinket, a tiny winged figure darted into view — Alexandre Crystelfeu, though he insisted with a grin: “Friends call me Lex. Or handsome. Either works.” Sparkling tattoos glowed faintly on his arms as he hovered, peppering the party with fun facts and questions, his curiosity boundless. Though wary at first, Luna and Pip welcomed him warmly, and Lex happily declared himself their new traveling companion.
While touring the town in search of fun and shinny, the companions overheard a heated quarrel between Aldorren and Calvessan, a gnomish artificer with skin streaked in green and orange. Calvessan accused him bitterly of being a fraud, claiming Aldorren’s dazzling “magic” was nothing but fireworks and tricks powered by his own devices. Stung, Aldorren stormed from the workshop, leaving the companions to probe Calvessan further.
Over cups of strong ale in the artificer’s cluttered shop, they learned that he and Aldorren had once been classmates in a “Philosophy of Charms and Incantations” course. Aldorren, ever the showman, had convinced him to craft trinkets for the academy’s theatre troupe, though Calvessan quickly abandoned the stage. He explained that artificers were builders of machines and illusions, more engineers than mages. Archivon, he admitted, had never held his interest — he was no mage, only a craftsman — but he recalled hearing Seraphius’s name whispered in the corridors, though they had never spoken.
Seeking answers and confirmations, the party tracked Aldorren down at the inn, where he brooded over a cup of wine. At first evasive, he finally confessed the truth: he was not a mage, not for lack of effort, but for lack of talent. He revealed that Seraphius had once been his mentor, though he was but one among many disciples and never managed to secure his master’s attention. Furious at his failures, Aldorren left Seraphius years ago. His attempts to reach out since had been met with silence.
And yet, Aldorren clung to hope. “Archivon,” he insisted, “is my chance. When I find its secrets, Seraphius will have no choice but to acknowledge me.” He vowed to continue northward, convinced the lost city’s mysteries would finally grant him the recognition he craved.
The companions weighed their options. They could follow Aldorren north, or seek Seraphius, or investigate the old academy where Aldorren once studied. After debate, they chose Mistbarrow, setting their sights on the academy by boat.
Before leaving, Pip was challenged to fetch a rare mushroom in exchange for his beloved rotten persimmons. But the task broke his heart: to succeed, he would have to kill the mushroom. Tearful, he prepared to do it — until Luna reminded him of her vivarium spell. With her help, Pip preserved the mushroom instead, who became his loyal Shroompanion. The persimmons were his prize, but the true treasure was his new companion.
