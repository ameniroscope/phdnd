Chapter IV — The Academy in the Mist
Beyond the storm-torn coast of Eidoria, where the sea whispers old secrets against the cliffs, lies a village suspended between sleep and legend: Mistbarrow. Few travelers arrive by choice. Fewer still arrive drenched, exhausted, and fresh from battle.
Yet it was upon these rocks, beneath clouds black as iron, that Pip, Luna, and Lex first beheld the lights of the village — flickering lanterns glimmering faintly through the thinning fog. Injured, trembling, and worn by the serpent’s wrath, they sought only shelter from the unrelenting storm.
What they found instead… was destiny.
The cliffs offered a single hope: a cave mouth opening like the jaw of some slumbering creature. Inside, the storm’s fury faded to a distant murmur. Warmth — unnatural warmth — brushed their skin. The faint scent of smoke lingered in the still air.
As their lantern light stretched deeper into the cavern, a shape rose before them like the side of a hill. A mound of bronze scales. A coiled tail thicker than an oak trunk. And at its heart, resting upon colossal claws… A sleeping dragon.
Even in slumber, it radiated a presence that pressed against the bones. Steam curled from its nostrils in soft, rhythmic exhalations. Near its claws lay a scatter of oddments: trinkets, tools, lost gear — a humble hoard built from travelers too wise (or too frightened) to retrieve what they’d dropped.
The companions knew danger well. They also saw great opportunity.
And so, with steps careful as drifting snow, they lifted what fate had conveniently placed within reach: a brass compass and a scythe for Pip, a dagger and a matchbox full of unspent sparks for Luna and a candle with a crossbow for Lex. Not treasure stolen… but gifts borrowed from chance.
The dragon’s eye opened for a heartbeat — golden, ancient, unbothered — then closed again.And the three fled into the dawn.
Morning broke pale and silver as they descended toward the village. Mistbarrow, with its crooked chimneys and moss-laden roofs, seemed carved from the fog itself. Yet at its center rose something far older: an edifice of stone arches and stained glass, its spires lost in the drifting haze. Mistbarrow Academy. The last surviving refuge of scholars who believe knowledge is worth bleeding for.
As Pip, Luna, and Lex approached the great archway, a rumble of stone signaled their greeting. Two weather-worn gargoyles stirred from slumber — Grumble and Rumble, guardians of the gate who bickered like brothers. Grumble lectured. Rumble complained. Both warned, quite animatedly, that the visitors were standing in the wrong place, asking the wrong questions, and — unintentionally — that the academy basement held the summoning circle where Seraphius had cast his final spell.
(They also warned not to touch windows. Or anything. Or everything.)
Before the pair could devolve further into argument, the doors burst open to reveal Hilda Fennwick, keeper of keys, commander of tours, and perhaps the only person capable of silencing two enchanted stone creatures with a single glare.
She ushered the weary trio inside, launching into the grand tour. Through vaulted halls glowing with lantern light, she guided them: The Great Hall, echoing with laughter, clattering mugs, and the faint smell of roasted nuts. The Greenhouse Laboratories, warm with alchemical steam and humming fungi. The Dormitories, where living students and restless spirits shared uneasy harmony. The Library, a cathedral of shadow and ink — its forbidden section veiled behind a velvet curtain. The Long Corridor, where armor muttered about old battles. The Headmaster’s Office, sealed behind polished oak and far too many locks. And finally, the heart of student life: the small coffee bar nestled beneath the hall’s arches.
It was here they first met Petra — quick-smiled, quick-talking, smelling faintly of smoke and mint. She poured steaming drinks, gossiped brightly of forbidden topics, and spoke with an almost delighted fear of a local cult hunting a mythical “Meaning Page.”
Then, with a conspiratorial grin, she passed them a hand-drawn map. And invited them to class.
In the greenhouse laboratory, under the pulse of glowing mushrooms and the hiss of cauldrons, Petra placed simple gray stones into their hands.
“Stubborn little things,” she said. “But if you coax them… oh, they sing.”
With grinding mortars, shimmering infusions, and a flame that burned blue as glacier-light, the stones melted and reformed — reshaping themselves into crystalline polyhedrons.
Their own alchemy-forged dice, thrumming faintly with potential. A gift of craft.  A mark of initiation. A step deeper into the arcane.
When the lesson ended, the companions’ fates were already tangled in the academy’s.
Exploration carried them next to the creaking dormitory halls, where a soft blue glow revealed Bran, a timid ghost-child clutching an overdue book on mushroom taxonomy. His shame was palpable.  His library card missing. His relief immense when the party vowed to return the book for him.
The library accepted the offering. In return, the shelves yielded secrets:
 • a spell written in invisible ink,
 • a recipe in indecipherable runes,
 • notes pointing toward an ancient summoning ritual.
Their search carried them through abandoned classrooms, rumors of student plots, and finally to the locked, silent office of the Headmaster, where a cryptex, star chart, and strange alchemy symbols awaited them.
Still, one clue remained elusive. The answer came from a suit of armor.
Clanking, boastful, and entirely convinced of his own legend, the enchanted armor relived its imaginary campaigns with gusto. Between tales of goblins wielding baskets and Thellerkin warriors offended by footwear, he revealed two truths: The Headmaster’s favorite word lay upon a window touched by south-west light. The Thellerkin forest folk once rescued Archivon knowledge — and still guard what remains.
The basement beckoned. Down into cold stone corridors they ventured, until they stood before the statue of a woman holding a star — the mother of Seraphius, carved by her grieving son.
The cracked summoning circle hummed faintly with lingering magic. They spoke the spell. The air shimmered. And the spirit of Seraphius Eldrin emerged.
Tall. Tired. Gentle. A scholar bound by failure and hope in equal measure.
He told them of his searches — of mountains scorched by forgotten fire, of traces of Archivon’s great library fused into stone, of a mother who vanished chasing a dream.
And he warned them:
“Three paths lead to Archivon.
 One through ash,
 one through forest,
 one through water.
And the Cult of the One Word seeks to claim what they do not understand.”
Then he was gone. Leaving only the cold.
Crawling back to the surface, the party finally tackles the mystery of the forbidden books. Their final answers waited in the shadows. By gathering sigils etched beneath plant pots, the companions unlocked the velvet-shrouded alcove. Within, dust swirled around a single sealed tome.
And from the spirit that lingered near Luna like a whisper of memory, the riddle came. By counting stained glass, stone watchers, and owls perched in their own home, the seal broke.
Inside lay a poem — a lament of Lake Virienne, where Archivon’s core fell and the city sank beneath the waters. A truth long forgotten. A truth reborn in their hands.
When they returned to the Great Hall, Petra greeted them with worried eyes and whispered warnings: the cult was watching the lakes, lurking in forests, searching the mountains — converging upon Archivon’s mystery.
And so, with the academy’s secrets gathered, the way forward branched:
The Path of Ash
To the scorched valley where Seraphius believed Archivon burned.
The Path of Forest
Into Thellerkin lands, where living trees guard old knowledge.
The Path of Water
Toward Lake Virienne — and the drowned shadow of a vanished city.
The Path of Conflict
For the cult stirs in the dark, hungering for a single page that holds the meaning of life.
The companions stepped out into the mist, the academy fading behind them like a dream — but the hunt for Archivon had only just begun.
