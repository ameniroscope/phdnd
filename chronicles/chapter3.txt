Chapter III – The Silver Current
Morning broke soft and gray over Fenkrey. The town stirred beneath a thin veil of mist — gulls crying over the rooftops, wagons creaking down cobbled streets, and the metallic clatter of merchants setting up for market day. Within the inn, silence lingered, heavy with the memory of last night’s revelation. Aldorren was gone. His lies, once gilded in charm, now hung hollow in the air. The companions found only his half-finished pie, a cold cup of tea, and the faint imprint of his boots pressed in the mud outside. No letter. No apology. Only the quiet certainty that their road no longer followed his.
Lex was the first to speak, wings twitching as he leaned casually against the table. “Funny story,” he began, tossing a coin and catching it with a grin. “Turns out lying makes consequences. I, uh… maybe liberated Aldorren’s coin pouch. Emotional compensation! We can call it truth tax, yes ?” Pip groaned, Luna sighed, but neither protested when the pouch spilled open — thirty gold coins gleaming in the pale light. Enough for new beginnings.
By midday, Fenkrey’s square had transformed into a whirl of color and sound. Brass pipes hissed steam into the air. Vendors shouted over one another, hawking charms, clocks, and candied nuts. A mechanical bird clattered across the rooftops, followed by a laughing child and the smell of oil and spice. Bells chimed from the crooked clocktower, never quite in rhythm with the music below.
Pip and Luna wandered through the maze of stalls, their coin purse clinking with possibility. They bought what they needed — and plenty they did not:
a flask of strong coffee said to “banish sleep and reason alike,”
food and fishing gear for the voyage,
a compass with a curious black stone that always rolled back toward its needle,
a deck of cards, a slingshot, and a mending scroll.
For Pip, Luna also purchased a vial of Sweet Spore Concoction, a brew designed to lull even the most restless traveler into dreamless slumber — just in case the lake journey grew too long for forest-loving hearts. Lex, meanwhile, vanished into the crowd and returned randomly with a suspiciously proud expression and two new knives tucked under his belt. “They were on sale,” he explained. No one believed him.
Among the stalls, Luna heard a soft, rhythmic tick-tick-tick that did not match any clock tower above. Beneath a curtain of hanging lanterns sat a tiny gnome in brass goggles, muttering over a pile of gears. When he saw the broken watch dangling from Luna’s belt, he nearly dropped his wrench. “By the gears! That’s Master Elorien’s work!” he cried. “He vanished years ago, chasing some shiny contraption into the forest. If you want that fixed, I’ll need his Heartgear. Bring it back, and I’ll make it sing again.”
The promise of knowledge — and the chance to repair another piece of the past — drew Luna at once. Pip and Lex, eager for adventure, followed without hesitation. Together they set off east, into the woods beyond Fenkrey’s gates.
The air grew thick with moss and damp earth. Mist gathered under the branches, and every sound seemed to echo a little too long. Soon, the group drifted apart amid the tangled roots and silver fog. Pip wandered down a slope carpeted in mushrooms. Tiny caps twitched as he passed, and faint giggles rose from the soil. “Sing-sing, spore-friend,” whispered a dozen little voices. “Wake the song that sleeps.”
Smiling, Pip hummed the tune that bloomed in his mind. The mushrooms swayed and glowed, their colors deepening with every note. When the final chord faded, he felt it settle into him — a living melody. Thus he learned the Song of Spores, a gentle tune that could soothe even the angriest heart — though each verse left him dizzy and spent.
Elsewhere, Luna followed a faint light through the mist until she reached a clearing where time itself seemed to pause. Floating in the center hung a lantern, soft and white, and within its flame danced a small, spectral figure no larger than her hand. “You carry fragments,” the spirit whispered. “Pages adrift. The library remembers its children, even torn from its roots.” Something in its voice stirred Luna’s memories of Archivon — that endless city of knowledge, now lost beneath the waves. With reverent hands, she carried the lantern close, promising warmth and purpose once more. Thus she gained the Librarian’s Lantern, whose whisper could recall forgotten truths when the world grew silent.
By dusk, the three companions reunited, weary but triumphant, with the gnome’s Heartgear clinking in Luna’s pack. True to his word, the tinker repaired the watch — and with a flourish, gifted her a curious compass that pointed not north, but toward whatever one missed most. Luna turned it in her palm, watching the needle tremble. For her, it pointed east — always east.
The next morning, Lex announced he had found a boat. “It moves on its own!” he declared proudly. “Mostly forward!” The others exchanged glances. Still, a vessel was a vessel, and soon they were gliding across the waters of Lake Virienne — a vast expanse of silver mist and mirrored skies. The world around them fell away until only the creak of wood and the hush of waves remained.
Days passed in gentle rhythm. Luna studied her spellbook on deck, Pip sketched mushrooms from memory, and Lex tried to fish with more enthusiasm than success. Then, one evening, the wind flipped open Luna’s book to a page written in old French — a language only Lex could read. Together they deciphered the forgotten script, their laughter echoing over the still water. The spells were faint, half-remembered things, but enough to awaken small elemental powers in each of them: Luna controlling water around her; Pip coaxed moss to shield his friends; Lex kindled fire along the edge of his wings. For the first time in weeks, hope shimmered brighter than fear.
But peace, as always, was fleeting.
It began as a ripple beneath the surface — then a shadow, then a roar. The lake split apart as a colossal serpent burst from the depths, scales glimmering green and silver, eyes like twin moons.
The battle that followed was chaos and courage intertwined. Pip, chanting his newfound melody, sent waves of confusion through the beast’s mind. Luna’s spells flared and faltered, her final mend saving the boat from splintering apart. Lex darted between the serpent’s thrashing coils, knives flashing — until the creature’s tail sent him flying into the water with a splash and a shout. But the tide turned. Pip swung his staff like a hero of legend, striking true. The serpent fell back into the depths with a shudder that rippled across the lake, leaving behind only stillness — and a single scale, gleaming with stored light. Exhausted and victorious, Pip drank his Sweet Spore brew and fell instantly into blissful sleep. Lex, soaked and breathless, salvaged what he could: a handful of scales, a curved horn, and his dignity. Luna, cradling her glowing lantern, watched the light of the serpent fade beneath the waves.
Dawn rose pale and cold. Mist clung to the shore as ruined towers emerged from the fog — ivy climbing their broken spires, bells tolling faintly in the distance though no hands moved them. As their battered boat scraped against the dock, the companions read the inscription carved into weathered marble: “Academy of Mistbarrow — In Pursuit of the Infinite Word.” Luna’s compass quivered, its needle spinning wildly. Pip stirred from his dreams, and Lex gazed at the misted halls with wide, eager eyes. Somewhere within those ruins, truth awaited — and perhaps the next page of Archivon’s story.
