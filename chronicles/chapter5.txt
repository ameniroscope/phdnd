Chapter V — Quiet Fires, Lingering Eyes
Mistbarrow did not release its guests easily.
Even after the academy’s towers dissolved into fog and its bells fell silent behind them, something remained — a pressure at the edge of thought, like a book left open in another room. The companions carried with them poems half understood, warnings spoken by ghosts, and the uneasy knowledge that they had been noticed. Knowledge, once stirred, rarely slept again.
Seeking distance from stone and scholars alike, they made camp at the forest’s edge. There, where moss softened the ground and the academy’s glow faded into lantern-light among the trees, they built a small fire and let its warmth claim the night. The forest was quiet — not empty, but listening. An owl called from the branches above, its cry slow and thoughtful, as if weighing their presence.
For a long while, no one spoke.
At last, Pip broke the silence.
He told them of a life once lived beneath the forest floor, in a burrow warmed by hearth-fire and song. His home had been no place of gloom, but of comfort —everywhere spores and roots, mushrooms tended with patience rather than command. He had lived as a hermit, content to cultivate living things and let time pass like drifting pollen.
That peace had not endured.
A darkness had crept into the woods, not a beast or a storm, but something quieter and more insidious. It whispered through bark and soil, poisoned the mycelium that bound the forest together. Pip had fought it with every charm and rite he knew, but knowledge failed him where fear did not. In the end, he left his burrow behind, entrusting the forest’s defense to Bramble Thornshield — a hedgehog warrior as steadfast as any knight — and set out in search of Archivon, where forgotten wisdom might yet save what he had lost. He spoke gently, but the firelight betrayed the truth beneath his words: Pip feared the dark, not for what it hid, but for what it might already have taken.
When Pip fell silent, Luna took his place beside the fire.
Her story was quieter still. Before the road learned her name, she had lived among shelves — borrowed libraries, damp monasteries, civic archives no one bothered to maintain. She copied texts by hand, restored pages others deemed worthless, catalogued fragments that survived only because she lingered long enough to notice them. When the coin ran thin, she brewed inks and tonics, traded careful spellwork for meals or passage. It was not ambition that guided her, but continuity.
Home, for Luna, had never been a single place. It was a narrow desk by a window, rain tapping against old stone, the scent of dust and boiled herbs in the air. It was the quiet permission to read until dawn. Family existed somewhere beyond that — practical, distant, loving in a way that never quite reached understanding. She had written them letters once. Then fewer. Then none at all. Not from cruelty, but from the quiet fear that she no longer knew how to explain who she was becoming.
Lex listened with a crooked smile that never fully settled.
When he spoke, his words came lighter, but no less sharp. He had been born to silk and silver, to a life already decided for him. When he refused it — when he chose clever hands and quicker wits over titles and obedience — disappointment followed like a shadow. University had been his escape, and he never returned when his family made their judgment clear. Thievery, roguery, freedom — all had come easier than forgiveness.
The fire burned lower. The forest leaned closer.
Sleep did not come easily that night.
Long after the others had drifted into uneasy rest, Pip remained awake beneath a gnarled yew tree. With a small belt knife, he carved wood by lantern-light, shaping not mushrooms this time, but memory. A simple tripod emerged beneath his hands, like the ones once used in spring rites to hang kettles and herbs above communal fires. When it was done, he wrapped its legs in forest twine and whispered the lullaby the spores had taught him long ago. The wood did not glow, nor did it answer in any visible way — yet the night felt steadier for its presence.
The fire crackled.
Not from wind, but from cold.
The forest shifted, branches creaking as though drawn by a single breath. From the underbrush came movement — small skeletal figures no taller than a child’s knee, their hollow eyes glowing like embers on the brink of extinction. Behind them stepped a robed figure, masked and thin, its voice sharp as torn parchment.
“Seize the seekers,” it hissed. “Their questions echo too far.”
The attack was sudden and merciless. The companions rose quickly, but strength alone was not enough; blades struck bone where nothing vital remained. Yet where force failed, ingenuity prevailed. Burning coals were hurled into grasping limbs. Nets tangled brittle frames. Knives and stones found their way into empty eye sockets with terrifying precision. One by one, the skulkers fell apart into ash and fragments, until only two remained — retreating toward their handler, who fared no better.
Roots snared its feet. Blows rained down. And finally, Pip’s spores bloomed, clouding the figure’s mind in drifting hallucination.
Bound and drugged, the handler babbled — not to its captors, but to an imagined master. In its delirium, truths slipped free. Archivon, it claimed, held a single page — the Meaning Page — the secret of life itself. The cult despised the Thellerkin, forest-folk who guarded what they could not steal. Three fates for the lost city were whispered: burned to ash, hidden among the trees, or drowned beneath a silent lake. And beneath Mistbarrow Academy itself, the cult had carved out a lair — accessible, Pip dimly recalled, through a door in the kitchens.
When clarity returned to the handler’s eyes, mercy did not. A swift blow ended the questioning, and the companions fled before the forest could answer again.
By dawn, the camp lay abandoned.
Ash marked where a fire had burned. Footprints led back toward the academy’s shadow. Above, the trees stood unchanged — patient, ancient, watching.
Mistbarrow had given them fragments of truth.
The forest had given them warning.
And somewhere beneath stone and root alike, something old had begun to move.
